1. Introduction: The New Paradigm of Digital Lending
The landscape of credit risk assessment in India has undergone a seismic shift, driven by the
convergence of ubiquitous digital payments, stringent regulatory oversight, and the maturation
of artificial intelligence. The era of unregulated digital lending, characterized by opaque
algorithms, coercive recovery practices, and data sovereignty violations, has been decisively
ended by the Reserve Bank of India’s (RBI) Digital Lending Guidelines (DLG) of 2023. For
architects, data scientists, and product leaders, this necessitates a fundamental rethinking of
how lending platforms are conceived, engineered, and operated.
Building a credit risk project today is no longer merely a data science challenge; it is a complex
orchestration of legal compliance, ethical AI, and robust software engineering. The modern
lending platform must solve the "thin-file" problem—assessing the creditworthiness of the
hundreds of millions of Indians who transact digitally via the Unified Payments Interface (UPI)
but lack a formal credit history (CIBIL score)—while simultaneously adhering to rigid standards
of explainability and privacy. The traditional "black box" model, which outputs a score without
justification, is functionally obsolete and legally perilous in a regime that demands transparency
for every adverse action.
This report provides an exhaustive, step-by-step technical guide to constructing such a platform.
It moves beyond high-level abstractions to detail the specific architectural patterns, feature
engineering techniques, and algorithmic strategies required to build a system that is not only
predictive but also compliant, fair, and transparent. We will explore the integration of Gradient
Boosted Decision Trees (GBDTs) with monotonic constraints for regulatory safety, the
deployment of SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) for generating adverse action codes, and
the use of DiCE (Diverse Counterfactual Explanations) to provide customers with actionable
paths to approval. Furthermore, we will detail the full-stack engineering required to deliver this
intelligence via an offline-first Progressive Web App (PWA) tailored for India's variable network
conditions.

2. Regulatory Compliance as Architecture
In the current ecosystem, compliance cannot be an afterthought; it must be the foundation of the
system architecture. The distinction between Regulated Entities (REs)—banks and
NBFCs—and Lending Service Providers (LSPs)—fintech platforms—dictates the flow of funds,
data, and liability.

2.1 The Direct Fund Flow Mechanism
The most critical architectural constraint imposed by the RBI is the prohibition of "pass-through"
accounts. Historically, fintechs would pool borrower repayments into their own nodal accounts
before settling with the lender. This practice is now explicitly banned. The architecture must

enforce a direct flow of funds between the borrower and the RE.

2.1.1 Engineering the Bypass Architecture
To implement this, the backend microservices cannot process funds directly. Instead, the
payment gateway integration must be configured in a "Nodal Bypass" or "Direct Settlement"
mode.
●​ Disbursement: When a loan is approved, the Loan Origination System (LOS) sends a
signal to the RE’s Core Banking System (CBS) or a connected payment switch. The
transfer is initiated directly from the RE’s regulated bank account to the borrower’s
validated bank account. The fintech platform receives only a status webhook
(Success/Failure/Pending).
●​ Repayment: Similarly, repayment mandates (e-NACH or UPI Autopay) must be
registered in favor of the RE. The fintech app acts solely as a UI layer for initiating the
transaction, but the beneficiary VPA (Virtual Payment Address) or account number must
belong to the lender.
Implications for Database Design: The transaction ledger in the fintech’s database must be
strictly a "Shadow Ledger." It records the intent and confirmation of transactions but does not
serve as the legal record of funds. Reconciliation services must be built to constantly sync this
shadow ledger with the RE’s bank statements to detect discrepancies, but the system must treat
the RE’s record as the single source of truth.

2.2 Data Sovereignty and the DPDP Act
The Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act 2023 introduces stringent requirements for
Data Fiduciaries. The architecture must enable granular consent and absolute data sovereignty.

2.2.1 Data Localization Strategy
All customer data must reside on servers physically located within India. This requirement
extends beyond the primary database to include backups, logs, and analytics warehouses.
●​ Cloud Selection: If using hyperscalers like AWS or Google Cloud, resources must be
strictly pinned to the ap-south-1 (Mumbai) or ap-south-2 (Hyderabad) regions.
●​ Disaster Recovery (DR): Cross-region replication for DR purposes must be contained
within Indian borders. Replicating data to a Singapore or Frankfurt region for redundancy
is a violation of the DLG.

2.2.2 The "Purpose Limitation" Architecture
The system must be designed to purge data once its specific purpose is fulfilled. This is a
radical departure from the "store everything forever" philosophy of big data.
●​ Retention Policies: The system must distinguish between "KYC Data" (which must be
retained for statutory periods, typically 5-10 years under PMLA rules) and "Alternative
Data" (e.g., SMS logs used for underwriting). Once the loan decision is made, if the user
is rejected, the granular SMS logs should ideally be purged or anonymized, retaining only
the derived score, unless specific consent is maintained for future assessments.
●​ Revocation Workflows: The backend must expose an API endpoint for consent
revocation. Triggering this endpoint must initiate a cascade deletion process across all

microservices—removing the user’s data from the marketing service, the underwriting
cache, and the analytics data lake within a legally defined timeframe.

2.3 Key Fact Statement (KFS) Engine

a latency constraint: the pricing engine must calculate the final risk-based interest rate, add all
processing fees, verification charges, and third-party costs, and compute the APR in real-time.

3. Data Strategy for Thin-File Borrowers
In the absence of robust credit bureau data (CIBIL/Experian) for "New-to-Credit" (NTC)
populations, the platform must construct a "synthetic" credit profile using alternative data. This
requires a sophisticated data ingestion and feature engineering pipeline that transforms
behavioral footprints into credit signals.

3.1 The Hierarchy of Alternative Data
We can categorize alternative data based on its predictive power and accessibility. The
architecture must prioritize sources that offer the highest signal-to-noise ratio while remaining
compliant with privacy norms.

3.1.1 Unified Payments Interface (UPI) Transaction Logs
UPI data is the gold standard for income estimation in the informal economy. Unlike bank
statements which can be infrequent, UPI logs capture high-velocity, low-value transactions that
reveal daily cash flow patterns.
●​ Income Stability Analysis: The system must parse narratives to identify recurring
inflows. In the gig economy, "salary" is rarely labeled as such. Instead, the system looks

for "Cluster Consistency"—recurring payments from the same VPA (e.g., a Zomato or
Swiggy payout wallet) arriving at similar intervals.
●​ Network Centrality: By analyzing the P2P (Person-to-Person) graph, the system can
calculate the user's "Eigenvector Centrality." A user who transacts with other highly-rated
users or established merchants is statistically less likely to default than a user who
transacts in a closed loop with known defaulters.

3.1.2 SMS Parsing and Financial Hygiene
While the RBI prohibits accessing personal messages, reading transactional SMS (with explicit
consent) remains a primary method for scraping financial data on Android devices in India.
●​ Regex Engines: The system utilizes complex Regular Expressions (Regex) to parse
standardized SMS formats from banks (e.g., "Acct XX123 debited for Rs 500").
●​ Feature Extraction: The goal is to construct a "Virtual Bank Statement." Key features
include the "Average Daily Balance" and the "Ratio of Insufficient Funds (NSF) Alerts." A
user who frequently receives "Low Balance" warnings via SMS presents a high liquidity
risk, regardless of their total income.

3.1.3 Utility and Bill Payment Data
Data from the Bharat Bill Payment System (BBPS) acts as a proxy for "Willingness to Pay."
Regular payment of electricity, water, and mobile postpaid bills indicates organizational
discipline.
●​ Latency Proxy: The system calculates the gap between the "Bill Generation Date" and
the "Bill Payment Date." A user who consistently pays on the due date (Gap = 0) is
reliable; a user who pays 3 days late (Gap = -3) shows signs of stress or disorganization.

3.2 Feature Engineering for the Informal Sector
The raw data must be transformed into mathematical features that a GBDT model can ingest.
For the Indian informal sector, standard features like "Debt-to-Income Ratio" are often calculable
only through proxies.

3.2.1 Volatility and Stability Indices
Income volatility is the enemy of repayment. A gig worker earning ₹50,000 one month and
₹5,000 the next is higher risk than a salaried worker earning a steady ₹20,000.
●​ Coefficient of Variation (CV): We calculate the CV of monthly inflows over a 6-month
window (CV = \frac{\sigma}{\mu}). A lower CV indicates stable income.
●​ Sawtooth Analysis: We analyze the daily balance curve. A "Sawtooth" pattern—where
funds arrive and are immediately withdrawn to near zero—indicates a lack of savings
buffer. The "Time to Zero" (average days from income receipt to zero balance) is a critical
predictive feature.

3.2.2 The Affordability Index
Instead of a simple Debt-to-Income ratio, we construct an "Affordability Index."
●​ Disposable Income Calculation: \text{Estimated Income} - (\text{Rent Proxy} +

\text{Utility Avg} + \text{Existing EMI Proxy}).
●​ Rent Proxy: Identified by large, recurring monthly debits to individual accounts.
●​ Existing EMI Proxy: Identified by keywords like "Bajaj", "Muthoot", "HDB" in transaction
logs.
●​ Logic: If the requested EMI > 40% of the calculated Disposable Income, the system flags
the application as high risk, regardless of the user's raw income.

4. Machine Learning Model Development
The core decision engine utilizes Gradient Boosted Decision Trees (GBDTs). While Deep
Learning (Neural Networks) excels at unstructured data (images, text), GBDTs like XGBoost,
LightGBM, and CatBoost remain superior for tabular financial data due to their handling of
discrete features, missing values, and interpretability.

4.1 Algorithm Selection: LightGBM
LightGBM is often preferred in production environments over XGBoost for two reasons: speed
(leaf-wise growth) and efficient handling of large datasets.
●​ Handling Missing Data: Thin-file borrowers will inherently have missing features (e.g., no
utility bill history). LightGBM handles NaN values natively by learning the optimal direction
(left or right split) for missing entries during training, which is far superior to arbitrary
mean/median imputation which distorts the data distribution.

4.2 Monotonic Constraints for Regulatory Safety
A critical, often overlooked requirement in credit modeling is Monotonicity. In a regulated
environment, the relationship between specific risk factors and the output score must be
directionally consistent. It is legally indefensible to reject a user because their income increased,
yet complex non-linear models can sometimes learn such spurious correlations due to noise in
the training data.
Implementation Strategy: We explicitly enforce monotonic constraints during model training.
●​ Income Features: Constraint = +1 (As Income increases, Score/Probability of Approval
must increase).
●​ Delinquency Features: Constraint = -1 (As number of past defaults increases, Score
must decrease).
●​ Utilization Features: Constraint = -1 (As credit utilization increases, Score decreases).
Code Structure for Constraints: In the LightGBM configuration, we pass a vector of
constraints:
# 1 = Increasing, -1 = Decreasing, 0 = No Constraint​
monotone_constraints = [1, -1, -1, 0, 1,...]​
model = lgb.LGBMClassifier(monotone_constraints=monotone_constraints)​
This ensures that the model's decision surface creates a "fair" and justifiable policy, preventing
"black box" anomalies where better financial behavior leads to worse outcomes.

4.3 Probability Calibration

The raw output of a GBDT classifier is not a probability; it is a log-odds score (logit). To perform
risk-based pricing (setting the APR based on risk), we need an accurate probability of default
(PD).
●​ Calibration Technique: We utilize Isotonic Regression. Unlike Platt Scaling (Sigmoid),
which assumes a specific S-shaped distribution, Isotonic Regression fits a non-parametric
stepwise function. This is ideal for credit scoring where the distribution of risk scores is
often skewed.
●​ Evaluation Metric: The model is optimized not just for AUC-ROC (ranking quality) but for
Brier Score (calibration quality). A well-calibrated model ensures that among 100
applicants predicted to have a 10% default risk, close to 10 actually default. This accuracy
is vital for financial forecasting and provisioning.

5. Explainable AI (XAI) and Adverse Action Generation
The RBI guidelines and global best practices (like the Equal Credit Opportunity Act in the US)
mandate that lenders provide specific reasons for denial. This requires unwrapping the "Black
Box" of the ML model.

5.1 SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)
We employ SHAP, a method rooted in cooperative game theory, to attribute the model’s final
score to individual feature contributions.
●​ The Concept: For a specific prediction, SHAP calculates the marginal contribution of
each feature value towards the output, compared to a baseline.
●​ TreeExplainer: We use shap.TreeExplainer, which is an optimized, exact algorithm for
tree-based models (like LightGBM). This is significantly faster than the model-agnostic
KernelSHAP, making it feasible for real-time inference.

5.2 The Reason Code Generation Pipeline
Raw SHAP values (e.g., income_feature: -0.45) are unintelligible to consumers. We must
convert these into "Adverse Action Codes" or "Reason Codes."
Step-by-Step Algorithm:
1.​ Compute SHAP Values: For the rejected applicant, generate the vector of SHAP values.
2.​ Filter Negative Contributors: Isolate features that pushed the score down (towards
rejection).
3.​ Rank by Magnitude: Sort these negative features by their absolute SHAP value (impact).
4.​ Semantic Mapping: Map the technical feature names to regulatory-approved explanation
strings using a lookup table.
○​ utilization_30d \rightarrow "Ratio of balance to credit limit is too high."
○​ inq_6m \rightarrow "Too many recent applications for credit."
○​ upi_volatility \rightarrow "Income sources appear irregular."
5.​ Selection: Select the top 3-4 reasons to display in the rejection letter/email.

6. Customer Recourse: Counterfactual Analysis
While SHAP explains why a rejection happened, it does not tell the user how to fix it. To
empower the customer (and improve future conversion), we implement Counterfactual
Explanations.

6.1 DiCE (Diverse Counterfactual Explanations)
We integrate the Microsoft DiCE library to generate actionable "What-If" scenarios.
●​ The Optimization Problem: DiCE solves an optimization problem to find a perturbed
feature vector x' such that the model predicts "Approve" for x', while minimizing the
distance between x' and the original input x.
●​ Diversity: DiCE generates multiple diverse paths. For example, Path A might involve
increasing income, while Path B involves reducing debt. This gives the user agency to
choose the path that suits their situation.

6.2 Implementation Constraints for Realism
Generating counterfactuals blindly can lead to absurd advice (e.g., "Change your age to 45" or
"Reduce your number of children").
●​ Immutable Features: We explicitly configure DiCE with a list of immutable features (Age,
Gender, ZipCode, History_of_Defaults). The algorithm is constrained to only vary mutable
features like Current_Balance, Utilization, or Recent_Inquiries.
●​ Feasibility Constraints: We also impose range constraints. We typically constrain
income increases to reasonable bounds (e.g., +20%) to avoid suggesting impossible
salary jumps.
●​ Actionable Advice: The system translates the valid counterfactual into a user-friendly
prompt: "If you pay down ₹5,000 of your current credit card debt, your approval probability
increases to 85%.".

7. Full-Stack Engineering Architecture
The deployment architecture must support high concurrency, strictly segregate data for security,
and function reliably on the unstable networks typical of Tier-3 Indian cities.

7.1 Microservices Design with FastAPI
The backend is built using Python’s FastAPI framework. Python is chosen to minimize the
"impedance mismatch" between the Data Science (ML) environment and the Production

Application environment.
●​ Service Decomposition:
○​ Gateway Service: Handles auth, rate limiting, and request routing.
○​ LOS (Loan Origination Service): Manages the state machine of the loan
application (Draft -> Submitted -> Underwriting -> Approved/Rejected).
○​ Inference Service: A dedicated, scalable container that hosts the LightGBM model,
SHAP explainer, and DiCE engine. Isolating this is crucial because explainability
computations are CPU-intensive and should not block the I/O-bound API traffic.
●​ Asynchronous Processing: FastAPI’s async capabilities are leveraged to handle
external API calls (e.g., fetching a CIBIL report or scraping SMS data) without blocking
the main thread.

7.2 Database Schema and Data Lifecycle
We utilize PostgreSQL as the primary transactional store.
●​ Schema Design for Compliance:
○​ users: Stores PII (Encrypted at rest).
○​ loan_applications: Stores the state and KFS snapshot.
○​ consent_ledger: A dedicated table tracking every permission granted (purpose,
timestamp, revocation_status).
●​ Data Partitioning: To comply with retention policies, data is often partitioned by time.
This makes it efficient to drop old logs (e.g., drop partition sms_logs_2020) when the
retention period expires, ensuring compliance with the DPDP Act’s storage limitations.

7.3 Offline-First Frontend with Next.js (PWA)
The user interface is built as a Progressive Web App (PWA) using Next.js.
●​ Service Workers for Offline Resilience: We use service workers (next-pwa) to cache
the "App Shell" (HTML/CSS/JS bundles). This ensures the app loads instantly, even on
2G networks or in airplane mode.
●​ Background Sync: If a user completes an application while offline, the data is serialized
and stored in the browser’s IndexedDB. The Service Worker listens for the sync event
(when connectivity returns) and silently pushes the payload to the backend. This prevents
data loss in areas with spotty internet.
●​ UI Performance: We utilize lightweight component libraries like Mantine or Chakra UI.
These libraries are tree-shakable (only used code is bundled), minimizing the JavaScript
payload size, which is critical for low-end Android devices common in the target
demographic.

7.4 Security Architecture
●​ Encryption: All sensitive fields (PAN, Aadhaar) are encrypted at the application level
before insertion into the database using AES-256.
●​ API Security: The internal ML service is not exposed to the internet. Communication
between the Gateway and the ML Service happens over a private Docker network.
External traffic is guarded by strict CORS policies and Rate Limiting (using Redis) to
prevent scraping or DDoS attacks.

8. Operationalization and Monitoring
Deploying the model is merely the beginning. Financial behavior is dynamic, and models
degrade over time.

8.1 Drift Detection and Retraining
The system must monitor for two types of drift:
●​ Data Drift (Covariate Shift): Has the demographic of applicants changed? (e.g., younger
applicants entering the pool). We use the Population Stability Index (PSI) to monitor
feature distributions. A PSI > 0.2 triggers an alert.
●​ Concept Drift: Has the relationship between features and risk changed? (e.g., high
income becoming less protective due to inflation). We monitor the Target Distribution
(default rates) in real-time.
●​ Retraining Pipeline: When drift is detected, an automated pipeline (Airflow/Kubeflow) is
triggered to retrain the LightGBM model on the most recent window of data, validating the
new model against the holdout set before promoting it to production.

8.2 Fairness Auditing
We implement a Disparate Impact Analysis module. This scheduled job (running
weekly/monthly) compares the approval rates across protected classes (Gender, Location). If
the approval rate for a protected group falls below 80% of the reference group (the "Four-Fifths
Rule"), the system flags the model for a bias audit, prompting data scientists to investigate
potential algorithmic bias.

9. Conclusion
Building a digital lending platform for the Indian market requires a synthesis of advanced
technology and deep regulatory awareness. By leveraging UPI-based feature engineering, the
system solves the access problem for thin-file borrowers. By integrating monotonic GBDTs,
SHAP, and DiCE, it solves the transparency problem, transforming the "black box" into an
explainable, compliant decision engine. Finally, by adopting an offline-first PWA architecture,
it ensures that this sophisticated financial tool is accessible to the users who need it most,
regardless of their device or network quality. This blueprint represents not just a technical
specification, but a roadmap for responsible, inclusive financial innovation in the post-DLG era.

Public-Facing Explainable Credit Risk Engine (India) – Project Overview
Objective: Build a web-based prototype credit-scoring service for Indian lenders that ingests
borrower data from users (via forms or uploads), scores credit risk with machine learning, and
provides transparent explanations for each decision. This PoC uses public Kaggle datasets (e.g.
Home Credit Default Risk, bank transaction data) to train models like gradient-boosted trees
and small GRU/LSTM networks. The system is containerized (Docker) with a Next.js frontend
and a RESTful backend API. Crucially, every decision is accompanied by an explanation (SHAP
feature contributions, “reason codes,” and counterfactual suggestions) to meet India’s regulatory
emphasis on transparency and fairness.
Architecture & Key Components
The system comprises the following layers and services:
Frontend (Next.js Web UI): A responsive, mobile-first interface collects borrower inputs (e.g.
personal/profile data, income, liabilities) and displays results. The UI adapts to Indian conditions
– it supports Hindi/English, simple layouts and even feature-phone/USSD-style fallbacks. After
scoring, it shows the approval/denial outcome, detailed factor contributions, and “reason codes.”
It also clearly discloses loan terms (interest rates, fees) in compliance with RBI transparency
rules.
Backend API (Python/Flask or FastAPI, Dockerized): Exposes endpoints for data submission,
scoring, and explanation retrieval. All logic (data validation, feature lookup, model inference)
runs here. The API is containerized for portability and can be orchestrated with tools like Docker
Compose or Kubernetes. Sensitive data is handled per Indian privacy laws (e.g. DPDP 2023) –
we avoid storing unnecessary personal data and encrypt any logs.
Orchestrator / MLOps Pipeline: A workflow engine (e.g. Apache Airflow, Prefect or Kubeflow)
automates data training pipelines. On a schedule or triggered by new data, it reads cleaned
Kaggle datasets and any aggregated user data, performs feature processing, retrains models,
calibrates probabilities, and publishes new model versions. Logging ensures auditability (model
versions, data used) for governance.
Data & Feature Store: Raw input and intermediate features are stored in a database (e.g.
PostgreSQL or NoSQL). A feature store (e.g. Feast or a custom service) serves consistent
features to training and serving. The store holds engineered attributes (see below), so that the
same values (and pre-processing logic) are applied at inference time. Historical raw data is
retained (with PII stripped) for offline analysis and model retraining.
Model Stack: The core ML models may include tree-based ensembles (LightGBM/CatBoost) for
tabular data and a small GRU/LSTM network to capture any sequential behavior (e.g. monthly
transaction or payment history). We enforce monotonicity constraints on key features where
appropriate (to aid interpretability) – for example, higher income should not reduce score. All
models output a default probability that is then calibrated (e.g. via isotonic regression) to align
with true risk.
Explainability Module: A dedicated component computes explanations for each prediction. We

use model-agnostic tools like SHAP to break down a score into feature contributions. For each
applicant, the module identifies the top contributing factors and generates simple “reason
codes” (e.g. “High existing debt,” “Low income,” “Very new credit file”). We also generate
minimal-change counterfactuals: for a denial, we compute the smallest adjustment (e.g. income
+20%) that would flip the decision. These explanations are returned via the API and shown on
the frontend in human-friendly language.
Storage & Audit: All decisions and inputs are logged for audit. This includes storing
(anonymized) applicant profiles, model version, score, and explanation vectors. This audit trail
aligns with RBI’s guidance that AI decisions be auditable and transparent. Data retention
respects DPDP and RBI IT Guidelines (no excessive collection).

Data Ingestion & Feature Engineering
Training Data: We leverage publicly available datasets for initial model building. For example,
the Kaggle Home Credit Default Risk dataset includes borrower demographics, credit history,
and past application records. We also use bank transaction datasets to simulate
spending/income patterns. These provide features like credit bureau scores, past delinquency
flags, and aggregate credit usage.
User Input Ingestion: Borrowers (or lenders on their behalf) can submit new data via an online
form or CSV upload. The frontend sends this to the API, which validates types and ranges. We
mimic typical loan-app data: age, employment type, income, past defaults, etc. Missing or noisy
fields are handled by imputation (mean/median or predictive imputation) as in our Kaggle
training pipeline.
Feature Engineering: We construct derived features to capture risk signals. This includes
common financial ratios (e.g. debt-to-income, credit utilization) and time-series summaries (e.g.
number of months since last delinquency). Categorical variables (occupation, education) are
encoded (one-hot or embeddings). We also apply transformations (binning or Box-Cox) to
stabilize skewed data. Where appropriate, unsupervised techniques (PCA/autoencoders)
reduce dimensionality. High-cardinality features (e.g. employer name) use target-based
encodings or embedding layers. As in credit modeling practice, each engineered feature is
documented with monotonicity (“higher is better” or vice versa) to aid explanations.
Feature Store: Computed feature values are stored centrally so that the online scoring service
fetches exactly the same values as used in training. This avoids “training-serving skew.” The
store maintains both raw and normalized values; the API queries this store when scoring
incoming requests.

Model Training & Explainability
Model Training: The training pipeline (orchestrated offline) fits the models on historical data. We
use gradient-boosting models (LightGBM/CatBoost) due to their strong performance on tabular
data. A GRU network is trained on any available temporal sequence (e.g. monthly payments) to
detect late-payment patterns. We perform cross-validation and hyperparameter tuning for

accuracy. After initial training, we calibrate predicted probabilities (e.g. isotonic regression) so
that scores correspond to true default rates.
Explainability: Following RBI’s FREE-AI recommendations, we integrate explainability tools into
the pipeline. For each prediction:
SHAP Values: Compute SHAP values for the chosen model to quantify each feature’s positive
or negative contribution. These are visualized as bar charts or waterfalls on the UI.
Reason Codes: We extract the top-N SHAP features as plain-language reasons (e.g. “Income:
₹30,000 vs typical ₹50,000; Credit card balance high”). This mirrors industry practice (Equifax’s
model produces personalized reason codes).
Counterfactuals: If an applicant is denied, we generate a counterfactual explanation: *“If your
annual income were ₹X instead of ₹Y (and all else equal), you would have been approved”*.
This is done with a technique like DiCE or custom search for minimal changes.

Global Interpretability: We also track global feature importance and partial dependence curves
to ensure the model aligns with domain knowledge (e.g. monotonicity). We will manually inspect
these during validation to detect any bias or unexpected behavior.
Regulatory Compliance: By design, all explanations are logged and linked to the model version,
fulfilling requirements for an audit trail. The system can output model diagnostic reports on
demand, as RBI suggests (using tools like SHAP or LIME) to demonstrate fairness and lack of
bias.

Indian-Specific Design Considerations
This proof-of-concept is tailored for India’s lending context:
Informal Economy: Over 90% of Indian workers are in informal employment. Income can be
volatile and undocumented, so our model uses proxy signals (e.g. length of tenure, borrowing
history). We allow users to enter income as a range or seasonal values, and create features
capturing income stability.
Digital Access: Roughly half of India’s mobile users still have feature phones. While our PoC is a
web app, the UI is designed mobile-first with large inputs and local-language support. In future,
the same scoring API could back voice- or SMS-based front ends (USSD/IVR) for
non-smartphone users.
Regulatory Transparency: RBI’s Digital Lending guidelines mandate that lenders explain key
terms (interest, fees) and credit decisions to customers. Thus the UI will clearly display these
elements alongside the score. The model itself is designed to minimize hidden biases: we follow
RBI’s “auditable algorithm” advice and avoid using sensitive attributes.
Inclusion: RBI requires that even borrowers with no formal credit history be gradually brought

into the system. Our prototype accommodates “thin-file” cases by relying more on alternative
data (transactions) and providing provisional scores. We do not connect to real credit bureaus in
the PoC, but our feature set mimics bureau features where possible (e.g. count of past loans).
Ethical AI: We heed RBI’s emphasis on fairness and ethics. During validation, we will check for
disparate impact (e.g. no unfair denials for any community) and aim to mitigate biases by
adjusting training weights or models. This aligns with RBI’s requirement for “fairness by design.”
Data Privacy: The design avoids unnecessary personal data. We comply with India’s DPDP Act
(2023) and RBI’s call to “avoid over-collection”. All user data is encrypted at rest, and the
service could be deployed on-premises at a lender if needed.

Deployment Format
The system is fully containerized for easy deployment. The backend API and explainability
service run in one or more Docker containers, and the Next.js frontend is served from another
container. For example, a Kubernetes cluster (or even a single Docker Compose setup) can
host the containers, with environment variables controlling model paths. Model artifacts and
feature-store snapshots are versioned (e.g. with MLflow or Git LFS) and mounted into the API
container. A continuous integration pipeline can rebuild and redeploy the API whenever a new
model is trained. This Docker-based approach ensures the PoC can be demonstrated on any
cloud or on-prem machine with minimal setup.
Intended Use Cases for Indian Lenders
This prototype serves as an analytics and decision-support tool for lenders, particularly:
Digital Lenders/NBFCs: Fintech lenders can use it to pre-screen small personal or business
loans where quick credit checks and clear explanations improve customer trust.
Commercial Banks: As a supplement to formal credit bureau scores for salaried and
self-employed customers, especially in rural or semi-urban branches.
Microfinance & Co-operative Banks: Assessing underbanked borrowers (e.g.
micro-entrepreneurs) by incorporating alternate data and transparent rationale.
Risk Analysts / Regulators: As a sandbox to study how AI models make loan decisions, to
validate compliance with RBI’s “Explainable AI” expectations.
In all cases, the engine is not a final authority but a decision aid: it can flag high-risk applicants
or justify loan pricing, while lending officers review the reasons codes and counterfactuals
before acting.

Limitations of the PoC Design
Data Validity: Models are trained on Kaggle/benchmark data, which may not fully reflect current
Indian borrower behavior. Performance on real Indian data may differ.

No Live Integrations: This prototype does not connect to actual credit bureaus, bank systems, or
identity systems (Aadhaar, PAN). In practice, those integrations (and strict KYC/AML checks)
are needed.
Scalability: The PoC is designed for demonstration. It lacks a production-grade environment (no
cluster auto-scaling, limited fault-tolerance). High-volume throughput would need further
engineering.
Simplified UI/UX: The frontend is minimal and suited for demonstration. A real product would
require rigorous UX design for all user demographics (regional languages, low literacy, etc.).
Regulatory Validation: While we emphasize explainability, the model has not been vetted by RBI
or any authority. It should not be used for actual lending without thorough third-party validation
and compliance checks.
Model Risk: Like any ML model, ours can degrade over time (model drift) or exhibit unintended
biases. Ongoing monitoring and human oversight are required.

In summary, this project outlines a transparent, explainable credit risk engine prototype for the
Indian market. It combines a modern ML stack (GBMs, GRUs) with an emphasis on regulatory
compliance (SHAP explanations, reason codes, counterfactuals) and addresses local
constraints (informal incomes, feature-phone UI). While not production-ready, it provides a
concrete framework for lenders to experiment with AI-driven credit decisions under India’s
evolving regulatory landscape (e.g. RBI’s FREE-AI guidelines).
