1 Executive Overview & Context
1.1 The Problem: India’s Lending Gap
India faces a fundamental credit access crisis that has persisted for decades despite rapid
digital adoption. The paradox is stark: India has the world’s fastest-growing digital
payments ecosystem (UPI processes over 500 million transactions daily), yet 400 million
people remain functionally unbanked from a lending perspective.
Consider this scenario: Rajesh is a Zomato delivery agent earning 50,000/month
through UPI. His transaction history shows stable income, consistent merchant payouts,
regular bill payments, and financial discipline. Yet he has a CIBIL score of 0 (new to
credit). Traditional banks reject him immediately. He has no mechanism to prove his
creditworthiness.
The Core Problem:
•400 million unbanked/underbankedindividuals lack formal credit history. These
are not poor individuals—many earn sustainable incomes. But without documented
employment, they’re invisible to credit systems.
•90% of Indian workersin informal employment (gig, small business, agriculture).
Traditional lending was designed for salaried workers with ITR forms and salary
slips. It has no mechanism for the gig economy.
•$150 billion annuallyflows through UPI but creates no credit records. Every
transaction is a signal. Yet credit bureaus cannot access this goldmine of data.
•Black-box lending modelsreject borrowers without explanation. Even where
credit scoring exists, borrowers get vague reasons and zero recourse. The system is
opaque and unfair.
Why Traditional Infrastructure Fails:
1.Income Documentation Gap:Formal documentation unavailable for informal
workers. A salaried employee has salary slips. A gig worker has UPI transaction
history. Banks only understand the first.
2.Thin-File Problem:New-to-credit borrowers have no CIBIL history. They get
rejected before even being considered. The system bootstraps debt through preda-
tory lending.
3.Information Asymmetry:Lenders have all power. Borrowers have none. Hidden
fees, arbitrary rates, coercive recovery. No transparency.
4.Algorithmic Opacity:Even modern fintech apps use black-box models. Borrower
rejected with no explanation. How do they improve? They can’t.

2 Regulatory Architecture
2.1 Direct Fund Flow Mechanism (The “Shadow Ledger” Model)
The RBI explicitly prohibits pass-through accounts. Historically, fintechs would pool
borrower repayments into their own nodal accounts, then settle with lenders days later.
This created:
•Concentration risk (if fintech fails, borrower money is trapped)
•Potential for embezzlement
•Regulatory arbitrage (fintech controls funds, not regulated entity)
Our Solution: Nodal Bypass Architecture
Disbursement Path:
1. User approved by ML model (score 0.72, above 0.50 threshold)
2. LOS sends approval signal with loan amount to Regulated Entity (RE)
3. RE’s Core Banking System (CBS) initiates direct fund transfer
4. Amount flows: RE’s bank account→Borrower’s validated bank account (DIRECT)
5. Fintech receives only webhook: “DISBURSEMENT SUCCESS” with txn ID
6. Fintech database records: intent, timestamp, confirmation
Repayment Path:
1. Borrower grants repayment mandate (e-NACH or UPI Autopay)
2. Mandate beneficiary = RE VPA/account (NOT fintech)
3. Fintech app provides UI for mandate registration
4. Monthly: Money flows RE account→Borrower account (auto-debit)
5. Fintech receives confirmation webhook
6. Fintech records in shadow ledger (intent + confirmation)
Key Innovation: Shadow Ledger vs. Legal Ledger
Fintech maintains a “Shadow Ledger” in PostgreSQL that records:
•Transaction intent (approved disbursement of 10,000 on Jan 15)
•Confirmation status (SUCCESS/FAILURE/PENDING)
•Timestamp
•User ID
This shadow ledger is NOT the legal record. The legal record is RE’s bank statement.
Reconciliation service compares shadow ledger against RE’s actual bank transactions
daily. If discrepancies found, alerts data team. RE’s bank statement is always source of
truth.
Why This Matters:If fintech app crashes, data corruption, or bankruptcy, the
RE’s bank statement proves what happened. Borrowers’ funds were never at risk.

3.3 UPI Transaction Analysis: Income Proxy
The Challenge:Gig workers don’t have “salary.” They have Zomato payouts,
Swiggy payouts, Uber earnings, P2P money transfers. How do we identify real

Our Solution: Cluster Consistency Analysis
We scan 6 months of UPI transactions looking for:
1.Recurring Inflows:Same VPA paying at regular intervals (e.g., Zomato
pays every Friday)
2.Consistency Check:Amount varies (800-1200) but frequency is regular
3.Merchant Classification:Is it from a known payment processor? (Zomato,
Swiggy, Uber, NEFT from business)

3.4 Network Centrality: Social Proof
Innovation:Build a transaction graph and calculate borrower’s position in the
network.
Theory:Users who transact with well-established, highly-rated merchants/users
are lower risk than users in closed loops with known defaulters.
Implementation:
1. Build directed graph: User→Counterparties
2. Calculate Eigenvector Centrality: A measure of influence/connection quality
3. Interpretation: High centrality = transacts with trustworthy nodes = lower
default risk
Example:
•User A: Transacts with Flipkart, Amazon, HDFC Bank, Swiggy (established
entities) = High centrality = Lower risk
•User B: Transacts with same 3 individuals repeatedly (closed loop) = Low
centrality = Higher risk

MeanMonthlyIncome

5 Machine Learning Model Architecture
5.1 Algorithm Selection: Why LightGBM
The Decision:We chose LightGBM (Light Gradient Boosting Machine) over
alternatives (XGBoost, Neural Networks, Logistic Regression).
Why NOT Neural Networks:
Neural networks excel at unstructured data (images, text, audio). For tabular
financial data with:
•Hundreds of features, thousands of samples
•Missing values (thin-file borrowers lack some data)
•Mixed feature types (continuous income, categorical employment)
•Need for interpretability
Neural networks are over-engineered and under-interpretable. They learn spurious
patterns. They’re black boxes (bad for regulatory compliance).
Why NOT XGBoost:
XGBoost is powerful but slow. For large datasets (millions of applications), training
is expensive. LightGBM uses leaf-wise tree growing (vs. level-wise in XGBoost),
making it 10x faster.
Why LightGBM:
1.Native NaN Handling:Learns optimal split direction for missing values.
No imputation needed. Preserves data distribution for thin-file borrowers who
inherently have missing data.
2.Speed:Leaf-wise growth makes training 10x faster than XGBoost. For
monthly retraining pipelines, this matters.
3.Categorical Support:Native handling of categorical features (employment
type, gender, location). No need for one-hot encoding explosion.
4.Interpretability:Tree structure enables SHAP explanations (faster than
model-agnostic methods). Essential for regulatory compliance.
5.Regularization:Built-in regularization prevents overfitting on thin-file bor-
rowers (sparse feature space).

5.2 Monotonic Constraints: Enforcing Fairness
The Problem:Complex non-linear models can learn spurious correlations from
noise.
Example: If training data has noise where women with high income defaulted more
(random chance), the model learns: “High income + Woman = Lower approval.”
This is biased, incorrect, and legally indefensible.
Our Solution: Monotonic Constraints
We explicitly enforce that relationships between features and risk must be mono-

6.1 SHAP: The Theoretical Foundation
Foundation Theory:Cooperative game theory. Each feature’s “contribution” to
final score is its marginal value in the coalition of all features.
Intuition:Imagine a coalition of workers. One person’s “contribution” is how
much less the team produces if they’re removed.
In credit scoring: Income’s “contribution” to default risk is how much the model
changes if income value changes.

8 Operationalization & Monitoring
8.1 Drift Detection: Two Types
Data Drift (Covariate Shift):
Question: Has the distribution of applicants changed?
Signals:
•Younger applicants entering pool (median age drops from 35 to 25)
•More women applicants (50% to 70%)
•Different geographic distribution
•Different employment types
Detection: Population Stability Index (PSI)
PSI=X
(Recent%−Training%)×ln(Recent%/Training%)
Interpretation:
•PSI<0.1: No significant drift
•0.1<PSI<0.25: Moderate drift (monitor)
•PSI>0.25: Significant drift (retrain urgently)
Concept Drift:
Question: Has the relationship between features and risk changed?
Signals:
•Inflation: High income no longer protective (50K income is now low)
•Economic shock: Unemployment spikes, default rates increase
•Seasonal trends: Gig income drops during monsoon
Detection: Monitor actual vs. predicted default rates
•If 1000 applicants predicted 5% default risk, expect 50 to default
•If only 20 default: Model over-pessimistic (concept drift)
•If 100 default: Model under-pessimistic (concept drift)
